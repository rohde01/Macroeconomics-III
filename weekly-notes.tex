\section{Week 1}

\subsection{Assumptions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Time is discrete (i.e., not continuous) - only two moments matter, today and tomorrow. no time in between. only these two snapshots
    \item There is a representative agent. - One person represents entire economy. everyone is identical, thus studying one person means studying the entire economy. 
    \item Two period model $t \in\{0,1\}$ Captures the key tradeoff (consume now vs. later) without adding complexity. there is only today and tomorrow
    \item Agents discount the future at factor $\beta$ - How much you value future consumption relative to today. example: $\beta = 0.96$ means 1 unit of consumption tomorrow = 0.96 units today
    \item The agent can save in bonds $\left(a_1\right)$ which pay interest rate $R(=1+r)$
    \item Each period, agents receive an endowment $y_t$, which is known. Key: You can't change your income, only decide how to spend/save it
    \item The utility function is given by $u\left(c_t\right)$ - measures happines :-) consuming $c_1$.
    \\
    \\
No general equilibrium yet
    \item Assume $R$ is given exogenously ("Partial equilibrium"). You can't affect it—you're a price-taker.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Solving the Two-Period Problem }
\textbf{idea:} 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Consume more today → less savings → less to consume tomorrow
    \item Consume less today → more savings → more to consume tomorrow
\end{itemize}
We want to maximize total utility across both periods:
$$
\begin{array}{rl}
\max _{c_0, c_1} & U=u\left(c_0\right)+\beta u\left(c_1\right) \\
\text { subject to } & c_0+a_1=y_0 \\
& c_1=y_1+(1+r) a_1
\end{array}
$$
The two constraints are period 0 and 1 budget constraints. \textbf{problem}: We have 3 choice variables ( $\mathrm{c}_0, \mathrm{c}_1, \mathrm{a}_1$ ) and 2 constraints. \textbf{Solution:} combine the two constraints (see written notes for calculations):
$$
c_0=y_0-\frac{c_1-y_1}{1+r}
$$
This equaiton now basically says: "how much can i consume today if i want to consume $c_1$ tomorrow." the fraction on right side tells how much savings need to carry into period 1 to meet period 1 consumption demands. we can substitute the combined constraint into the utility function to obtain a simpler max problem: 
$$
\max _{c_1} U=u\left(y_0-\frac{c_1-y_1}{1+r}\right)+\beta u\left(c_1\right)
$$
To find the maximum we take the derivative of $U$ with respect to $c_1$ and set it equal to zero. after rearranging this gives us (see written notes for calcs):
$$
\beta u^{\prime}\left(c_1\right)=\frac{1}{1+r} \cdot u^{\prime}\left(c_0\right)
$$
The first order conditionis is the equation you get when you take the derivative of your objective function and set it equal to zero to find the maximum. When you're maximizing something (like utility), the FOC tells you where the peak is ie. where you can't improve by moving in either direction. The above equation can be re-arranged into the euler equation: 
$$
u^{\prime}\left(c_0\right)=\beta(1+r) u^{\prime}\left(c_1\right)
$$
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{left side:} marginal utility of consuming one more unit today
    \item \textbf{right side:} marginal utility of consuming one more unit tomorrow adjusted for impatience $\beta$ and interest $1+r$
\end{itemize}
The euler equation basically say: "at the optimum, the marginal benefit of consuming today equals the marginal benefit of saving and consuming tomorrow"
If this weren't true, you could make yourself better off by shifting consumption between periods $\rightarrow$ thus you have not found the optimal bundle. additionally we can rearrange like this:
$$
\frac{u^{\prime}\left(c_0\right)}{\beta u^{\prime}\left(c_1\right)}=1+r
$$
\textbf{The left side} is the marginal rate of substitution between consumption today and tomorrow. \textbf{The right side} is the market exchange rate between today and tomorrow (the interest rate). At the optimum, these must be equal.

To fully solve the problem we need:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The Euler equation: $u^{\prime}\left(c_0\right)=\beta(1+r) u^{\prime}\left(c_1\right)$ (This tells you the optimal ratio between $\mathrm{c}_0$ and $\mathrm{c}_1$)
    \item The budget constraint: $c_1=y_1+(1+r)\left(y_0-c_0\right)$ (tells you feasible combinations)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Utility function}
crucial assumptions on what makes a good utility function. we need properties that make economic sense: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Positive but Decreasing Marginal Utility. More consumption is always better. $u^{\prime}(c)>0$
    \item Decreasing marginal utility: $u^{\prime \prime}(c)<0$. Each additional unit brings smaller increases in happiness. This is why indifference curves are convex (curved inwards).
    \item The limit condition: $\lim _{c \rightarrow \infty} u^{\prime}(c)=0$ As you consume more and more, the marginal benefit approaches zero but will never be negative
    \item Consumption is Essential $\lim _{c \rightarrow 0} u^{\prime}(c)=\infty$. As consumption approaches zero (starvation), marginal utility explodes to infinity
\end{itemize}{}

\subsubsection{Constant Relative Risk Aversion (CRRA)}
The formula:
$$
u(c)=\frac{c^{1-\sigma}-1}{1-\sigma} ; \quad \sigma>0 ; \sigma \neq 1
$$
The workhorse utility function in economics adhering to all of the above properties. Taking the derivative:
$$
u^{\prime}(c)=\frac{d}{d c}\left[\frac{c^{1-\sigma}-1}{1-\sigma}\right]=\frac{(1-\sigma) c^{-\sigma}}{1-\sigma}=c^{-\sigma}
$$
we see the marginal utility is just:
$$
u^{\prime}(c)=c^{-\sigma}
$$

\textbf{The Parameter $\sigma$ (Sigma)}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Coefficient of Relative Risk Aversion: How much you dislike uncertainty/variability in consumption. High risk aversion $\rightarrow$ hate fluctuations, prefer smooth consumption.
    \item Intertemporal Elasticity of Substitution $=1 / \sigma$ This measures how willing you are to shift consumption between periods when the interest rate changes.
    \item As $\sigma \rightarrow \infty$, Leontief Preferences
\end{itemize}

\section{Week 2}

Two periods are not enough. We introduce an arbitrary, but finite horizon:
\paragraph{Preferences}
$$
U=u\left(c_0\right)+\beta u\left(c_1\right)+\beta^2 u\left(c_2\right)+\cdots=\sum_{t=0}^T \beta^t u\left(c_t\right)
$$
\paragraph{Dynamic budget constraints:}
$$
\begin{aligned}
&\begin{aligned}
& c_0+a_1=y_0+\left(1+r_0\right) a_0 \\
& c_1+a_2=y_1+\left(1+r_1\right) a_1
\end{aligned}\\
&\begin{aligned}
c_t+a_{t+1} & =y_t+\left(1+r_t\right) a_t \\
a_{T+1} & \geq 0
\end{aligned}
\end{aligned}
$$
\paragraph{problem components}
the problem then becomes:
$$
\max _{c_t, a_{t+1} \forall t} U=\sum_{t=0}^T \beta^t u\left(c_t\right)
$$
subject to: $$
c_t+a_{t+1}=y_t+\left(1+r_t\right) a_t ; a_{T+1} \geq 0 ; a_0=0
$$
\textbf{bottom line: }we're solving a household's consumption problem over multiple periods (from time 0 to time T). The household wants to maximize lifetime happiness by choosing how much to consume ( $c_t$ ) and save ($a_{t+1}$) each period. What limits choices are the budget constraint:
$$
c_t+a_{t+1}=y_t+\left(1+r_t\right) a_t
$$
this says: What you spend + what you save = what you earn + what you saved before (with interest). this problem can be setup and solved through \textbf{Lagrangian:}
$$
\mathcal{L}=\sum_{t=0}^T \beta^t u\left(c_t\right)+\sum_{t=0}^T \lambda_t\left[y_t+\left(1+r_t\right) a_t-c_t-a_{t+1}\right]+\mu a_{T+1}
$$
Instead of solving T separate optimization problems (one for each period), the Lagrangian lets us solve everything simultaneously by:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Taking derivatives with respect to all choice variables ( $c_t$ and $a_{t+1}$ for each $t$ )
    \item Setting these derivatives equal to zero
    \item This gives us the Euler equation and other optimality conditions
\end{itemize}
Euler equation is identical to 2-period model:
$$
u^{\prime}\left(c_t\right)=\beta\left(1+r_{t+1}\right) u^{\prime}\left(c_{t+1}\right)
$$
\paragraph{Infinite horizon}
The household now lives forever (or thinks infinitely far ahead):
$$
\sum_{t=0}^T \beta^t u\left(c_t\right) \rightarrow \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t u\left(c_t\right)
$$
This represents intergenerational altruism (parents care about children who care about their children, etc.)
\paragraph{no ponzi condition:}
Finite horizon had: $a_{T+1} \geq 0$ "cannot die with debt". Infinite horizon has:
$$
\lim _{T \rightarrow \infty}\left(\prod_{t=0}^T \frac{1}{1+r_t}\right) a_{T+1} \geq 0
$$
This is the discount factor that converts future assets to present value. The present value of your assets far in the future cannot be negative

You can borrow, but you must eventually pay it back (at least in present value terms). You can't roll over debt forever without eventually paying it back. The No-Ponzi condition is the infinite-horizon equivalent of "you can't die with debt." It prevents unrealistic Ponzi schemes where you borrow forever without repaying, ensuring the optimization problem has a sensible solution.
\subsection{Ramsey model}
The Ramsey model is essentially the Solow growth model with smart consumers. Instead of assuming people save a fixed fraction of their income, the Ramsey model lets people choose how much to save based on what's best for them.

\paragraph{key players}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Households: Choose consumption ( $c_t$ ) and saving ( $a_t$ )
    \item Firms: Use capital $\left(K_t\right)$ and labor $\left(L_t\right)$ to produce output using $Y_t=A K_t^\alpha L_t^{1-\alpha}$ Firms borrow capital from households and pays $r_t$ per unit of capital. 
\end{itemize}
\paragraph{key equations} Model boils down to these two equations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Resource constraint (law of motion for capital): $k_{t+1}=(1-\delta) k_t+f\left(k_t\right)-c_t$ this is how capital evolves: Capital tomorrow = leftover capital today (after depreciation) + new production - what we consumed. This describes total capital in the economy.
    \item euler equation: $u^{\prime}\left(c_t\right)=\beta\left(1+r_{t+1}-\delta\right) u^{\prime}\left(c_{t+1}\right)$ This balances "Should I consume today or tomorrow?". $r_{t+1}=f^{\prime}\left(k_{t+1}\right)=$ the return on capital (what you earn by saving)
\end{itemize}
\subsection{steady state}
The steady state is simply the long-run resting point of the economy - where nothing is changing anymore. Both consumption $c$ and capital $k$ have stopped moving. 
\paragraph{ss for capital accum.}In steady state, $k_{t+1}=k_t=k$ (capital stops changing). So simply substitute that into resource constraint:
$$
k=(1-\delta) k+f(k)-c
$$
solve for $c$:
$$
c=f(k)-\delta k
$$
This says: steady-state consumption = total output minus what you need to replace worn-out capital.
\paragraph{ss for euler equation}
In steady state, $c_{t+1}=c_t=c$ (consumption stops changing too). Substitute:
$$
u^{\prime}(c)=\beta(1+r-\delta) u^{\prime}(c)
$$
rearrange:
$$
r=\frac{1}{\beta}+\delta-1
$$
From the firm's optimization (slide 18), the firm hires capital until its marginal product equals the rental rate: $r=f^{\prime}(k)$
Substituting this into for $r$:
$$
f^{\prime}(k)=\frac{1}{\beta}+\delta-1
$$
this says: The marginal product of capital must exactly compensate households for waiting (impatience, captured by $1 / \beta$ ) and for depreciation ( $\delta$ ).
